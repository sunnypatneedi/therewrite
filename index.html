Module 01 / 12

The Great Transition

You've been told AI progress is about better models. That's roughly 15% of the story.

The frontier of artificial intelligence is not a single technology. It is a vertical stack of interdependent systems, each with its own physics, economics, and failure modes. A breakthrough in model architecture means nothing if the compute infrastructure cannot support training at scale. Cheaper inference means nothing if the silicon supply chain is bottlenecked at a single Taiwanese foundry.

This course maps the entire stack — from the power grid to policy frameworks — because the binding constraint shifts. In 2020, it was data. In 2022, it was compute. In 2024, it was energy and inference economics. By the time you ship a product, the bottleneck will have moved again. Knowing the full stack is how you anticipate where it moves.

The Stack Dependency Argument

Every layer in the frontier stack depends on the layer below it and constrains the layer above it. This is not a metaphor — it is a physical dependency chain. You cannot train a model without clusters. You cannot build clusters without chips. You cannot power chips without energy. Working backwards from the application layer through these dependencies is how systems engineers think about frontier AI.

But unlike a traditional software stack, frontier AI also has external constraint fields — safety requirements, capital markets, and geopolitics — that press against the stack from the outside, reshaping what is feasible at every layer. Ignoring these fields is how companies build technically brilliant products that cannot be deployed, funded, or legally operated.

Worked Example: What Happens When You Send a Prompt?

Request trace: You → Claude → Response
7
Application Layer
You type a prompt in the interface. The client sends an HTTP request to an API endpoint. Authentication, rate limiting, content filtering happen here.
6
Inference & Serving
A load balancer routes your request to an available GPU. The model weights (hundreds of GB) are already loaded in VRAM. Your tokens enter a batch queue. KV-cache manages context. Tokens are generated autoregressively — each one requires a full forward pass.
5
Model Architecture
The transformer processes your tokens through attention layers. Architecture decisions (context length, mixture-of-experts routing, layer count) determine quality and speed of the response you receive.
4
Training Systems
The model's capability was shaped months ago. Pre-training on trillions of tokens, RLHF alignment, safety tuning — all baked into the weights now being used. Training decisions you never see determine what the model can do.
3
Compute & Clusters
Your inference runs on one node in a massive cluster. The cluster's scheduling software decided which GPU handles your request. Network fabric connects thousands of GPUs for training. Your one request uses a fraction of one chip.
2
Silicon & Chips
The GPU running your inference was fabricated at TSMC months ago. Its architecture (H100, B200, TPU v5) determines the ops-per-second ceiling. Chip availability determined how many GPUs were allocated to serving vs. training.
1
Energy & Power
The data center powering that GPU draws from the regional grid. Cooling systems keep the chip at operating temperature. Your single request consumed roughly 0.001–0.01 kWh.
How much is 0.01 kWh, really?
⚡ A 10W LED bulb running for about 6 minutes
📱 Charging your smartphone for roughly 30 minutes
🔍 10–30× the energy of a single Google search
🌎 Now multiply by millions of daily requests — this is why hyperscalers are signing nuclear power agreements

Every prompt you send traverses this entire stack in milliseconds. The latency you experience, the quality of the response, the cost to the provider — all are determined by decisions made at every layer. Understanding the stack is understanding the real constraints of what you can build.

The primary bottleneck in frontier AI is:
Tap to reveal
Neither — it shifts.
The binding constraint has migrated from data (2020) to compute (2022) to energy and inference costs (2024). Systems thinking means tracking where the bottleneck is moving, not assuming it stays fixed. This is why you need to understand every layer, not just your own.
Inference costs are primarily determined by:
Tap to reveal
Both — model size AND hardware + optimization.
A larger model requires more compute per token, but hardware choice (GPU architecture, quantization, batching strategy) and optimization (KV-caching, speculative decoding, routing) determine the actual cost. You can cut inference costs 10x through engineering without changing the model.
A one-person frontier lab should focus on:
Tap to reveal
Application layer + fine-tuning.
Pre-training is a $100M+ capital expenditure. Inference infrastructure is a hyperscaler game. But the application layer — where domain knowledge meets UX meets model capability — is where a single builder with deep expertise can create more value than a team without it. Fine-tuning is your leverage point into the stack below.
